Summary

As the sole researcher for Slack's Enterprise Grid product line in the summer of 2017, my goal was straight-forward and focused: to give needed information to stakeholders so that informed decision-making was easy and prompt.

The experience goes way beyond what a typical internship offers: I was treated as a full time researcher and participated in every aspect of the business, including project kick-off, discussion and brainstorming, roadmapping, and evaluation and assessment, etc.

I am deeply proud of the work I've done at Slack, as well as the teams that I collaborated with in the process, including my fellow user researchers, the Enterprise design team, PMs and Enterprise Analytics team. The deep and rich relationships I developed with colleagues are my biggest treasures I obtained from this internship, along with the invaluable business communication and organization skills acquired from the fast-moving tech industry.

Communication for Collaboration

"Knowledge is nothing unless it is shared."

Smooth and effective business communication is the very foundation for my work at Slack. From setting meeting agendas to composing bi-weekly research precis for the rest of the company, I've learned the best way to generate user research impact is through transparency: people have easy access to my plans, roadmap, thoughts, progess and outputs,  as well as a channel to raise questions and concerns to me directly. 

Decks and Reports: Two Kinds

I produce two types of decks and reports: the first one I called "front-end" where stakeholders can quickly scan and extract information as they needed for their jobs. The second one, which I called "back-end", contains all details of research design and results, allowing audience to engage or be inspired at a deeper level. The “front-ends” are linked to the “back-ends” so stakeholders can choose information to read that suits their needs at the moment.

Centralized Place for Research Insights

A google site has been established internally by the User Research team during my time at Slack to present all research insights in one centralized place. The site contains sections that are corresponding to different product teams, serving as a knowledge bank for stakeholders from different areas to browse at any time. I have been managing research contents and roadmaps for Enterprise Grid product line.

Presenting with Impact

Besides providing actionable directions for the stakeholders, I’ve learned that the contents left out in a presentation are equally important, if not more important, than the contents included. The successfulness of a presentation may be measured by “Impact/min” — how much impact generated per a minute spent in the conference room. In this sense, understanding stakeholders needs go hand in hand with other preparation work such as rehearsal and timing.

Respect for Others' Time

My colleagues and stakeholders may happily accept the invitation to my presentation/coffee talk/lunch talk, etc., but it doesn’t mean they have unlimited bandwidth to sit in a room with me. Therefore, under the guidance of my mentor, I have developed a sense of respect for other people’s time. This applies to my practices including drafting meeting agenda and reducing number of formal meetings, etc.

Secondary Desk Research: Building Common Ground

The first major project after my arrival at Slack is summarizing all existing research related to Enterprise Grid. To be honest, it was quite challenging to perform such a duty as a new comer at the company, yet it turned out to be extremely fruitful.

The final deliverable is a presentation to the entire Enterprise Grid team, including executives, PMs, engineers, designers, marketers, data scientists, sales and customer support, etc. This desk research generates following impact:

Impact 1: Quick Vamp-Up

Reading and analyzing all research helps me to quickly understand and grasp the profession of business strategies and product development. This helps me to sync up with my stakeholders, and develops within me the mentality to help my team where they need me the most.


Impact 2: Avoid Reinventing the Wheel

Knowing what have been done aids me to prioritize my research agenda while planning the resources strategically — I dodge the traps to design new research that overlaps with past projects in terms of scope and data collection, and eventually save time to do more meaningful research.

Impact 3: Self-Introduction to My Team

The presentation also serves as my formal introduction to my team members. They gained a sense of what my strengths are, what my research roadmap looks like and how to reach me. All of which increase my accessibility to my teammates.

Impact 4: Cross-Functional Research Review

I reviewed and synthesized all relevant research, even those that were done by other teams, such as Analytics/Data Science, Marketing and Customer Support. For the first time, Enterprise Grid research was not organized by teams, but by topics that went beyond team boundaries.

Impact 5: Area of Opportunities

The rare opportunity to gain such a large amount of attention from the entire Enterprise team allowed me to effectively turn user insights from past research into recommendations and area of opportunities.

Overall, the desk research was tremendously beneficial to both myself, the User Research team, and the Enterprise product team I was embedded in. It conducted a common ground to discuss user-related subjects after the summarization of all past research.

Interviews: Understanding Contextualized Needs and Processes

Motivation

The Enterprise Grid product has grown exponentially since its launch in January. However, Slack has limited knowledge about some of the emerging problems. In this project, I explore the problem space, particularly regarding the user migration to Enterprise Grid, in the context of large cooperations.

Research Questions

1. What are Slack Enterprise users’ onboarding needs? In-app and beyond?
2. How are users using Slack Enterprise Grid? What features are they using regularly and/or are aware of?
3. What are the sentiment behind Grid usage?
4. [Stretch Goal] Any differences across Slack familiarity, job role, organization size & types?

Output and Impact

The final deliverable took form as a well-documented google doc file, as it was extremely convenient to access and share across different functional teams.

The report included highly scannable summary, as well as detailed findings in direct relation to the confusion observed from the users. It also revealed the organizational context regarding some of the causes of problematic migration to Enterprise Grid. The findings and suggestions helped the design team to gain more information of the use cases of Slack Enterprise, while adjust their roadmap to improve the onboarding experience of the software.

Usability Studies: User-Centered throughout Development Process

Concept Tests

Quickly help designers find the feasibility/validity of a few design thoughts, and to explore the follow up opportunities for the design, as well as questions to answer for the research.

Usability Tests

Formally access the performance of certain designs. Sometimes, tests go beyond just software prototypes to offer better reasoning and explanations on particular user behaviors.

“Rolling” Tests

At the end of month, anyone can sign up to have something tested, ranging from new design prototypes to soon-to-be shipped features. This facilitates a user-centric culture across all functional roles in the product team.

Tests That Welcome Everyone

No matter which type of test is about to be conducted, it will be marked in the corresponding calendar and is open to anyone at Slack to remotely observe. If someone misses the test, no worries, a debrief report will be available in a few days.

Survey & Statistical Analysis: Mining Hidden Details

Motivation

Usually one survey aims to answer one set of questions. But merging collected data and apply advanced statistical analysis can sometimes open up new doors to discover hidden details of the users.

Output and Impact

Besides practicing my solid knowledge of survey design, I discovered many of aspects of Slack Enterprise Grid users regarding their job functions and their work tool needs. Thanks to R, Python and some terrific packages for plotting and graphics, I was able to compose a report consisted of results of many exploratory data analysis and quickly share it with my fellow researchers and stakeholders.

The trick is not about how fancy the machine learning algorithm I used, or how pretty the charts are — though they will definitely help to convey visually pleasing messages. Instead, it is about giving just enough information to stakeholders to help them make informed decisions without getting confused or feeling pressured to ask more questions to clear up something simple, while learning interesting and easy to consume information.

Learning from Improving

"Every decision is a business decision." - April, VP of Product

One particular area that I improved was about composing concise, readable but also detailed and informative reports from depth interviews. Under the guidance of my lovely mentor and manager, I revised my report several times. Their constructive feedback help me to be even more aware of wording, and the implications that I did not anticipate. The feedback also helps me to avoid reporting every details that I find interesting, and to strengthen the focus of my reports on business considerations and bigger business pictures.

I am extremely grateful for my hosts. Not only did they help me to become a researcher who is able to strategically position research in the business environment, but I also learned to do meaningful user research in the fast-changing technology world. I know now that every research decision is a business decision.

Now, I am confident and ready to embrace any challenges that await.
