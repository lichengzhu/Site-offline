Mixed Methods Research:
Genesis of Organizational Form


"An excellent piece of exploratory research" - advisor

Summary

I designed and conducted this research from beginning to end to investigate how new organizational forms are developed. I selected Chinese environmental NGOs for my study because the field is relatively young, and is accessibility to me. Findings of this research lay a strong foundation for future research in many disciplines, particularly Sociology, Organizational Behavior, Social Networks and Management.

Through this project I have learned following:
    
1. New forms are not only results of organizations’ individual decision-making, but also due to external considerations.

2. Different types of organizational forms are associated with the       structural position one NGO possess in its organizational field.

3. Financial resources are one key factor that impacts on what kind of structure that a NGO develops. 


Main Takeways: international research under uncertain political environment

Flexibility of Research Design

The best research design is the design that fits the research subject and scope. Also, it is the research that produces maximum amount of insights with minimum resources. Mixing up different methods and flexibly choosing right methods at right moment is key to success of a research.

Safety and Sustainability

Participants need to be assured with a level of safety so that they can provide a steady source of information, while researchers need to make sure their personal safety is not at risk. This is especially important for certain exploratory research under potentially adverse environments.

Learning While Researching

Research, particularly product research, is not merely observing users from distance. It is non-linear and requires constant adjustments. It needs researchers to regularly reflect on research progress and direction, and pick up new tools and research knowledge should situations demand.

Grounded Truth

Exploratory research could be confusing and even misleading. Noise in collected data may cause interpretation and analysis fallible, and thus lose its reliability to guide follow-up research. In this case, researchers need to develop a good sense of “taste” based on grounded truth to distinguish noise from valid information.

Research Question

Both academic and professional behavioral/social/managerial scientists have long been examining the organizational constrains and influences — e.g. churches, schools, firms, military and governmental — on individual properties such as motivation, preferences, mentality and decision-making, etc. Yet, despite the universe of literature on the organizational impact on human behaviors, little has been done to understand how are these organizations formulated in the first place.

Following classical research tradition on the interplay between organizational structure and ecological conditions, I seek out to answer questions including: How is new organizational structure formed? How does organizational environment impact on organizational structural change? What mechanism(s), if there are any, play a part?

Participants & Recruitment Problems

Deciding Participants

Hence I selected Chinese environmental NGOs for my study for following reasons:

1. They are generally young, active and fast-changing, which is ideal to study the formation of new organizational forms.
2. I have volunteered at one organization a while ago, which made easier for me to approach them for close interactions and research.

Recruitment problems

When I set out to conduct exploratory interviews, to my surprise, I realized that most Chinese environmental NGOs are extremely hard to reach. As a result of the tightening governmental control and the fear of being targeted by the government, NGOs tend to be less willing to expose themselves in public media. At the same time, mass recruitment may attract unnecessary attention from the Chinese administration, which may hinder both safety of my research subjects as well as the safety for my own. Therefore, the first problem I need to overcome is how to collect quality data while maintaining a relatively low key. To summarize, the difficulties are:

1. Certain Chinese NGOs are reluctant to show too much media appearance due to political environment
2. Mass recruitment may pose risks for safety
3. Balancing data collection, project goal and local law/politics conformaty is crucial in China

Recruiting Interviewees in Steal Mode

After some hard thinking and a conversation with a native NGO leader in China, I narrowed down my strategy: If I can’t find those NGOs directly, I can let them come to me. I later discovered that NGOs occasionally hire volunteers and part-time employees on several online platforms, and this is when they surface. Hence I decided to find the contact information from those job postings. Doing so has several advantages:

1. Data sources on hiring platforms are centralized, and therefore it is easier for data collection.
2. Websites have clearer structure, and therefore it is easier for data collection.
3. Caught limited level of governmental attention.
4. Avoiding “dead” organizations who stopped operating years ago.

So I wrote a web crawler in python (code and collected data can be found on my GitHub account: https://github.com/lichengzhu/ChineseENGO) to scrape needed data from the web. I later compiled them into a list with contact information of discovered NGOs. 

Depth Interviews and Qualitative Data Analysis

With the name list, I managed to conduct 8 in-depth interviews with 8 NGO leaders. Using grounded theory, I extracted several common themes in relation to the factors that impact on the organizational forms of interviewed NGOs:

1. Registration status: if the organization has formally registered or not
2. Financial status: if the organization has sufficient money for registration
3. Information exchange: organization shares information with other NGOs
4. Support seeking: organization asks for help from other NGOs
Collaboration: organization collaborates with other NGOs on environmental-protection-related projects

It is interesting to see that, other than registration and financial status, the other three factors all to some degree involve inter-organizational relations among Chinese environmental NGOs. Therefore, I suspect that organizational forms of Chinese environmental NGOs were not only determined by internal considerations, but also exogenous factors. At this stage, it is clear that interviews on its own is not able to encompass those external elements. I need something that is able to capture social relations of Chinese environmental NGOs at a grander scale. Hence the survey. 

Survey and Inter-Organizational Relations

Using the insights from my exploratory interviews, I designed a survey to map out the inter-organizational relations among Chinese environmental NGOs. I sent out the survey to all organizations I found via the web crawler, and, surprisingly, most of the invitations bounced back.

Over 80% of the survey invitations were rejected by the email server on the other recipient side, claiming inactive accounts. I don’t know if this means that NGOs are purposely deactivating their accounts after staff hiring is over, due to the fear of being found. Or this simply means that those organizations stop operating and was shutdown. However, almost all NGOs who received my survey invitation completed the survey --- an extremely high response rate indicating their possible needs to be heard by third-parties.

With the survey, I managed to map out the relationships among Chinese environmental NGOs.

The graph of the multiple networks was constructed by four social relations among Chinese environment NGOs: Financial aid (NGOs who receive/give financial aid to each other), Information exchange (sharing information with each other), support network (When help is needed, which organization will be contacted) and collaboration (joint projects/programs). Visualization was made possible by muxViz version 1.0.5 (Domenico, Porter and Arenas 2015) https://github.com/manlius/muxViz. Each node represents an indiviadual NGO, and the vertrcies are directed. The multiplex networks in this graph were constructed in accordance with betweenness centrality of each node, on the basis of Fruchterman-Reingold force-directed layout.

Analysis and Preliminary Conclusions

With some statistical analysis, I was able to give following conclusions based on the data I have collected so far:

1. Financial resources are distributed extremely uneven among Chinese environmental NGOs.
2. The registration status of surveyed NGOs seems to be an independent phenomenon that could not be explained by other factors involved in the research.
3. NGOs who exchange information with each other tend to collaborate together, and vice versa (spearman inter-layer assortativity).
4. NGOs who are more “central” in the inter-organizational relations of Chinese environmental NGOs are more likely to be financially abundant than others (T-Test).

Field Work and Result Synthetization

The last, and perhaps the most interesting, step of my research is a two-month participatory observation at one of the environmental NGOs in Beijing, China. The leader of the NGO was kind enough to accept me as one of their summer interns, and did not restrict me at all, allowing me to participate in their meetings and their projects, and letting me observe and talk to their staff.

With such an opportunity, I was also able to observe my NGO’s interactions with other environmental organizations, as well as talking to their employees. The interview and the survey helps to direct my field work to focus on the inter-organizational aspects of the organizational processes, and to verify and elaborate on several insights I acquired from other parts of the research. Combining all pieces together, I concluded that:

1.  The major driving force of organizational forms are the financial resources available to a NGO and its relationship with other organizations in the field.
2.  There are two major forms of Chinese environmental NGOs, and I call them “product-oriented” and “project-oriented” (details in my paper).
The more “central” a NGO is, the more complexity in its form and structure.
3.  After the initial stage of birth, NGOs are starting to diverge and self-organize into different types, depending on the combination of their financial status and their network position. I call them “The hub,” “The businessman,” “Consultants of poor,” and “lone rangers” (details in my paper).

I further theorized and proposed two mechanisms to explain the observed phenomena of emergence of new organizational forms in the field of Chinese environmental NGOs: Comparative network advantage and Institutional ceiling. Though the proposed mechanisms go beyond the scope of an exploratory research, they help to tell a complete story of my research subjects while pointing to the hypothesis testing in the next stage of research.
